    How the METR is calculated

    The METR answers one question: if this investment earns a certain return before tax, how much of that return survives the tax system? The model builds two parallel cash-flow streams for the same project — one ignoring tax, one fully taxed — and compares the rate of return each one implies.

    1. Before-tax rate of return (BTROR)

    This is the return the project would earn in a world with no taxes at all. We build a 20+ year cash flow — the initial investment going out, operating income coming in every year, and the asset's resale value at the end — and find the single discount rate that makes that stream worth exactly zero today. That discount rate is the before-tax return.

    BTROR = IRR( real before-tax cash flow, year 0 to year N )

    This is the number shown in the "Real before-tax ROR" box on the right. IRR (internal rate of return) is solved numerically — there's no closed-form formula, the engine just tries rates until the stream's present value hits zero.

    2. After-tax rate of return (ATROR)

    Same project, same years — but now every tax in the system gets applied: corporate income tax, depreciation rules, import duties, dividend withholding, capital gains tax on the eventual sale. Whatever cash is left over after all of that is the after-tax cash flow. We find the discount rate that zeroes that stream out, the same way as before.

    ATROR = IRR( real after-tax cash flow, year 0 to year N )

    This is the number shown in the "Real after-tax ROR" box on the right. The after-tax cash flow already has income tax, depreciation effects, debt interest deductions, dividend tax, and capital gains tax baked into it — see the Income Tax, Losses & Credits, and Depreciation tabs for where each piece comes from.

    3. The METR itself

    The METR is simply the share of the before-tax return that tax eats up. If BTROR and ATROR are close together, taxes barely bite. If ATROR is much lower than BTROR — or even negative — the tax system is taking a large bite out of the project's return.

    METR = (BTROR − ATROR) / BTROR

    This is the big number at the top of the results panel. A METR of 0% means tax has no effect on this investment's return. A METR of 100% means tax absorbs the entire return. A negative METR means the after-tax return is actually higher than the before-tax one — usually a sign of a generous incentive (an investment tax credit or a tax holiday) more than offsetting the tax burden.
